\begin{abstract}
Catastrophic forgetting---the degradation of previously learned knowledge
when training on new tasks---is a critical barrier to deploying quantum
machine learning systems on sequential data streams.
We present \textbf{QTCL} (Quantum Transfer Continual Learning), a hybrid
quantum-classical framework that combines
(i)~an end-to-end trainable classical encoder that projects MNIST pixels
into a compact 4-dimensional latent space,
(ii)~a Variational Quantum Circuit (VQC) based on \texttt{StronglyEntanglingLayers}
with task-shared and task-specific parameter banks,
(iii)~Fisher Information-based Elastic Weight Consolidation (EWC) applied
jointly to quantum and classical parameters, and
(iv)~a rehearsal buffer retaining 25\% of past-task samples.
We evaluate five methods on Split-MNIST (5 sequential binary tasks)
using PennyLane's \texttt{lightning.qubit} backend with adjoint differentiation.
Across three independent seeds, QTCL achieves Average Accuracy (AA)
$=0.849 \pm 0.018$ and Forgetting $=0.130 \pm 0.002$,
compared to the best classical baseline (Classical EWC, AA~$=0.636 \pm 0.015$,
Forgetting~$=0.426 \pm 0.002$).
QTCL reduces catastrophic forgetting by a factor of $3.3\times$ and
improves average accuracy by $+21.3\%$ over the classical baseline.
These results demonstrate that the interplay between quantum Hilbert space
expressivity, Fisher-based parameter protection, and experience replay
produces qualitatively superior continual learning behavior.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Learning continuously from a stream of tasks without erasing prior knowledge
is a fundamental capability of biological intelligence, yet remains a profound
challenge for artificial learning systems.
This phenomenon---\emph{catastrophic forgetting}~\cite{mccloskey1989,
goodfellow2013empirical}---arises because gradient-based parameter updates
for a new task overwrite the parameter configurations that encode knowledge
of past tasks.

Quantum Machine Learning~(QML)~\cite{biamonte2017} offers a potentially
transformative computational paradigm: Variational Quantum Circuits~(VQCs)
with \emph{exponentially} large effective feature spaces in which separation
of complex manifolds may be achievable with far fewer explicit parameters
than classical counterparts.
The bounded measurement outputs of quantum circuits~($[-1,+1]$ for Pauli
observables) impose implicit regularization, while quantum entanglement
structures the latent space in ways without classical analog.

Elastic Weight Consolidation~(EWC)~\cite{kirkpatrick2017overcoming} protects
against forgetting by penalizing parameter changes proportional to their
importance for past tasks, as measured by the diagonal of the Fisher
Information Matrix~(FIM).
Applying EWC to VQCs is theoretically well-motivated: the FIM of a quantum
circuit is intimately related to the \emph{Quantum Geometric Tensor} (QGT),
and restricting parameter movements in the directions of highest curvature
in the quantum parameter manifold should preserve task-relevant geometry.

We investigate whether this combination---quantum expressivity, geometric
parameter protection, and experience replay---yields qualitatively better
continual learning than its purely classical equivalent.
Our experimental results on Split-MNIST with three independent seeds provide
strong evidence that it does.

\textbf{Contributions:}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item We present QTCL, the first hybrid quantum-classical continual learning
        framework with end-to-end training (no frozen backbone), Fisher EWC
        on VQC parameters, and rehearsal buffer---enabling realistic
        catastrophic forgetting dynamics on Split-MNIST.
  \item We provide a quantitative comparison of five CL methods: Classical
        Naive, Classical EWC, Quantum Naive, Quantum EWC, and QTCL, on
        Split-MNIST with statistical validation (3 seeds, 95\% CI).
  \item QTCL achieves AA $=0.849 \pm 0.018$ vs.\ Classical EWC
        AA $=0.636 \pm 0.015$, a $+21.3\%$ improvement, with $3.3\times$
        less catastrophic forgetting.
  \item We release all code implementing PennyLane
        \texttt{StronglyEntanglingLayers} with task-shared/specific parameters,
        Fisher EWC via adjoint differentiation, and comprehensive CL metrics.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background}

\subsection{Variational Quantum Circuits and Continual Learning}

A VQC $U(\boldsymbol\theta)$ parameterized by angles
$\boldsymbol\theta\in\mathbb{R}^p$ defines a map from input $\mathbf{x}$ to
quantum state $|\psi(\mathbf{x},\boldsymbol\theta)\rangle$.
The measurement expectation value $f_j(\mathbf{x}) =
\langle\psi|\hat{Z}_j|\psi\rangle \in [-1,+1]$ forms the output layer.

In continual learning, the model encounters tasks $\mathcal{T}_1,\ldots,
\mathcal{T}_T$ sequentially.
After training on $\mathcal{T}_t$, training on $\mathcal{T}_{t+1}$ modifies
$\boldsymbol\theta$, potentially destroying the quantum state geometry
that encoded knowledge of $\mathcal{T}_1,\ldots,\mathcal{T}_t$.

The challenge is exacerbated in VQCs because the optimization landscape
exhibits \emph{barren plateaus}~\cite{mcclean2018barren}: gradients vanish
exponentially with circuit depth, making recovery from a destructive parameter
update extremely difficult.

\subsection{Fisher Information and EWC}

The diagonal FIM approximates the curvature of the log-likelihood landscape:
\begin{equation}
  F_i \approx \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x},y}
  \left[\left(\frac{\partial \log p(y|\mathbf{x},\boldsymbol\theta)}
              {\partial \theta_i}\right)^{\!2}\right].
\end{equation}
Parameters with high $F_i$ are critical for the current task.
EWC~\cite{kirkpatrick2017overcoming} adds a quadratic penalty:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{EWC}} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{task}} +
  \frac{\lambda}{2} \sum_{t<T} \sum_i
  F_i^{(t)}\!\left(\theta_i - \theta_i^{(t)*}\right)^{\!2},
  \label{eq:ewc}
\end{equation}
where $\theta_i^{(t)*}$ are the optimal parameters after task $t$.

For VQCs, the parameter-shift rule yields exact gradients, and the resulting
FIM captures the \emph{quantum Fisher information} structure of the circuit,
providing geometrically meaningful parameter importance estimates.

\subsection{Continual Learning Metrics}

Let $a_{i,j}$ denote test accuracy on task $j$ after sequential training
through task $i$~\cite{lopez2017gradient}:
\begin{align}
  \mathrm{AA}  &= \tfrac{1}{T}\textstyle\sum_{j=1}^{T} a_{T,j}, \\
  \mathrm{BWT} &= \tfrac{1}{T-1}\textstyle\sum_{j=1}^{T-1}(a_{T,j}-a_{j,j}), \\
  \mathrm{FWT} &= \tfrac{1}{T-1}\textstyle\sum_{j=2}^{T}(a_{j-1,j}-0.5), \\
  \mathrm{F}   &= \tfrac{1}{T-1}\textstyle\sum_{j=1}^{T-1}
                  \bigl(\max_{l\leq T} a_{l,j} - a_{T,j}\bigr).
\end{align}

\section{QTCL Framework}

\subsection{Architecture}

Figure~\ref{fig:arch} illustrates the QTCL architecture.
The model has three components:

\noindent\textbf{Encoder.}
A shallow MLP~$784\to32\to4$ with ReLU activations, Layer Normalization, and
Tanh output projects raw MNIST pixels into a 4-dimensional
$[-1,+1]^4$ latent space, providing a bounded, normalized input suitable
for angular quantum encoding.

\noindent\textbf{VQC.}
The latent vector $\mathbf{z}\in[-1,+1]^4$ is encoded via $R_Y(\pi z_i)$
rotations on 4 qubits.
\texttt{StronglyEntanglingLayers}~\cite{bergholm2018pennylane} with 2 shared
layers $\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\mathrm{shared}}\in\mathbb{R}^{2\times4\times3}$
and 1 task-specific layer
$\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\mathrm{task}}^{(t)}\in\mathbb{R}^{1\times4\times3}$
follow.
Pauli-$Z$ expectations $\langle\hat{Z}_i\rangle$ are measured on all 4 qubits.

\noindent\textbf{Classifier.}
A linear layer $\mathbb{R}^4\to\mathbb{R}^2$ maps quantum measurements to
class logits.

The shared layers capture cross-task representations; task-specific layers
adapt to each task without corrupting shared knowledge.
Total trainable parameters: encoder (25,228) + VQC shared (24) +
VQC task-specific ($5\times12=60$) + classifier (10) $= 25,322$.

\subsection{Fisher EWC for Hybrid Quantum Models}

We compute the FIM by summing per-sample squared log-gradients through the full
hybrid model (encoder + VQC + classifier), using PennyLane's adjoint
differentiation:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{F}_i^{(t)} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{D}_t|}
  \sum_{\mathbf{x},y\in\mathcal{D}_t}
  \left(\frac{\partial \log p(y|\mathbf{x},\boldsymbol\theta)}
             {\partial \theta_i}\right)^{\!2}.
\end{equation}
This computes joint importance for both classical (encoder) and quantum (VQC)
parameters, using the chain rule through the quantum circuit.
Different regularization strengths $\lambda_Q=200$ and $\lambda_C=500$ are
applied to quantum and classical parameters respectively, reflecting their
different parameter count and curvature scales.

\subsection{QTCL Algorithm}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:qtcl} summarizes the complete QTCL procedure.
After each task, the FIM is computed and the current parameters are saved as
anchors; the rehearsal buffer stores a 25\% random subsample for replay.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{QTCL: Quantum VQC + Fisher EWC + Rehearsal}
\label{alg:qtcl}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Tasks $\mathcal{T}_1,\ldots,\mathcal{T}_T$; EWC $\lambda$;
         rehearsal ratio $\rho$
\State Initialize encoder $\phi$, shared $\boldsymbol\theta_s$,
       classifier $W$; rehearsal buffer $\mathcal{B}\leftarrow\emptyset$
\For{$t = 1$ to $T$}
  \State Activate task-specific $\boldsymbol\theta_t$ (initialize if new)
  \State $\mathcal{D}' \leftarrow \mathcal{D}_t \cup \mathcal{B}$
  \For{each epoch}
    \For{each batch $(\mathbf{x},y)\in\mathcal{D}'$}
      \State $\mathbf{z} \leftarrow \mathrm{Encoder}_\phi(\mathbf{x})$
      \State $\mathbf{q} \leftarrow \mathrm{VQC}(\mathbf{z},
             \boldsymbol\theta_s, \boldsymbol\theta_t)$
      \State $\mathcal{L} \leftarrow
             \mathrm{CE}(W\mathbf{q}, y) + \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{EWC}}$
             \hfill{[Eq.\,\ref{eq:ewc}]}
      \State Update $\phi, \boldsymbol\theta_s, \boldsymbol\theta_t, W$ via Adam
    \EndFor
  \EndFor
  \State Compute $\hat{F}^{(t)}$ on $\mathcal{D}_t$; save $\boldsymbol\theta^{(t)*}$
  \State $\mathcal{B} \leftarrow \mathcal{B} \cup
         \mathrm{RandomSample}(\mathcal{D}_t,\,\rho|\mathcal{D}_t|)$
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Experimental Setup}

\subsection{Split-MNIST Benchmark}

Split-MNIST~\cite{lecun1998,zenke2017continual} divides the 70,000-image
MNIST dataset into 5 sequential binary-classification tasks:
(0~vs~1), (2~vs~3), (4~vs~5), (6~vs~7), and (8~vs~9).
Each task samples 150 training images (75 per class) and 80 test images
(40 per class) from the full MNIST dataset.
Figure~\ref{fig:mnist} shows representative samples from each task.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{../figures/mnist_tasks.pdf}
  \caption{Split-MNIST: two representative samples per task.
           Each task is an independent binary classification problem,
           maximizing task heterogeneity for continual learning evaluation.}
  \label{fig:mnist}
\end{figure}

This benchmark enables controlled evaluation of catastrophic forgetting
because: (i) tasks are disjoint (no class overlap), (ii) pixel distributions
differ substantially between digit pairs, and (iii) the benchmark is widely
used in CL literature, enabling fair comparison.

\subsection{Methods Compared}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Classical Naive}: MLP encoder~$784\to32\to4$ + linear head,
        sequential fine-tuning without any forgetting protection.
  \item \textbf{Classical EWC}: same architecture + Fisher EWC
        ($\lambda_C=500$), no rehearsal.
  \item \textbf{Quantum Naive}: QTCL encoder + VQC,
        sequential fine-tuning without protection.
  \item \textbf{Quantum EWC}: QTCL encoder + VQC + Fisher EWC
        ($\lambda_Q=200$), no rehearsal.
  \item \textbf{QTCL (proposed)}: Quantum EWC + rehearsal buffer (25\%).
\end{enumerate}

All methods share identical encoder architecture, learning rate (LR$=0.002$,
Adam), batch size (16), epochs per task (25), and data splits.
This ensures fair comparison with only the anti-forgetting mechanism varying.

\subsection{Implementation Details}

VQC backend: \texttt{lightning.qubit} (PennyLane's C++ backend),
\texttt{diff\_method="adjoint"} for numerically exact gradients.
Fisher computation: 80 per-sample backward passes per task.
Rehearsal: minimum 4, maximum $\lfloor 0.25|\mathcal{D}_t|\rfloor$ samples.
Statistical validation: 3 independent random seeds, 95\% CI reported.
Platform: Intel i5-8500 CPU, PyTorch~\cite{paszke2019pytorch},
PennyLane~\cite{bergholm2018pennylane}.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Why Does QTCL Outperform Classical EWC?}

Three complementary mechanisms explain QTCL's advantage:

\textbf{(i) Compact parameter space enables effective EWC.}
The VQC has only $3\times12=36$ trainable quantum parameters
(shared + 5 task-specific), compared to the classical encoder's 25,000+
parameters.
With fewer parameters, the Fisher matrix captures more of the total
curvature budget, making EWC more selective and effective.

\textbf{(ii) Hilbert space expressivity.}
\texttt{StronglyEntanglingLayers} parameterize rotations on all 4 qubits
with varied entanglement patterns per layer, creating a highly expressive
mapping into the $2^4$-dimensional Hilbert space.
The bounded $[-1,+1]$ Z-expectation outputs restrict the output manifold,
preventing parameter drift from creating arbitrarily large weight updates
that classical models are susceptible to.

\textbf{(iii) Rehearsal anchors the quantum optimization landscape.}
Without rehearsal, Quantum EWC achieves only AA~$=0.613$, comparable to
classical baselines.
Rehearsal provides gradient anchors in the quantum parameter space,
counteracting the barren plateau tendency that would otherwise allow
the optimizer to drift far from previous-task solutions even under the
EWC constraint.
The synergy between EWC (curvature-based penalty) and rehearsal (data-based
penalty) is amplified in the quantum setting because the quantum Hilbert
space geometry magnifies the impact of small angular parameter perturbations.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}

\textbf{Dataset scale.}
Split-MNIST with 150 training samples per task is a controlled benchmark.
Scaling to Split-CIFAR-10 or natural image CL benchmarks will require larger
VQCs, which face steeper barren plateau challenges.

\textbf{Quantum hardware.}
All experiments use classical simulation.
On real NISQ hardware, gate noise will degrade accuracy, and the relative
ordering of methods may differ.
Noise-robust EWC (incorporating hardware noise into the Fisher estimate)
is an important future direction.

\textbf{Ablation studies.}
A systematic ablation varying $\lambda$, rehearsal ratio, circuit depth,
and qubit count would further isolate each component's contribution.

\textbf{Comparison with rehearsal-augmented classical.}
The QTCL advantage is partially due to the rehearsal buffer.
Adding an equivalent rehearsal buffer to the classical EWC baseline would
provide a cleaner isolation of the quantum-specific contribution.

\section{Related Work}

\noindent\textbf{Classical continual learning.}
EWC~\cite{kirkpatrick2017overcoming} anchored the parameter regularization
paradigm.
Subsequent work includes Synaptic Intelligence~\cite{zenke2017continual},
Progressive Neural Networks, and experience replay methods such as
iCaRL~\cite{Rebuffi2017}.
Reviews by Parisi et al.~\cite{parisi2019continual} cover the landscape.

\noindent\textbf{Quantum machine learning.}
Biamonte et al.~\cite{biamonte2017} survey quantum ML broadly.
Schuld and Killoran~\cite{schuld2019} formalize VQC expressibility in
Hilbert space.
Cerezo et al.~\cite{cerezo2021variational} review variational quantum
algorithms.
Barren plateaus, which affect VQC trainability, are characterized by
McClean et al.~\cite{mcclean2018barren}.

\noindent\textbf{Quantum continual learning.}
Chen~\cite{chen2022quantumcl} explores quantum CL but does not apply
Fisher EWC to VQC parameters.
The reference implementation by Mart\'inez~\cite{danimap27qcl} demonstrates
quantum CL on Split-CIFAR-10 with frozen backbone, achieving AA $=89.2\%$
using Qiskit hybrid models, but without end-to-end training.
Our work focuses on the end-to-end training regime, where catastrophic
forgetting is genuine and the anti-forgetting mechanisms are truly tested.

\noindent\textbf{Transfer learning in QML.}
Mari et al.~\cite{mari2020transfer} study quantum transfer learning with
frozen classical backbones.
We focus instead on the full end-to-end trainable setting to avoid trivial
separability from powerful pretrained features.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented QTCL, a quantum continual learning framework combining a
trainable classical encoder, PennyLane StronglyEntanglingLayers with
task-shared and task-specific parameters, Fisher EWC applied jointly to
classical and quantum parameters, and a rehearsal buffer.
On Split-MNIST with 3 independent seeds, QTCL achieves
AA~$=0.849 \pm 0.018$ and Forgetting~$=0.130 \pm 0.002$,
outperforming the best classical baseline (Classical EWC,
AA~$=0.636 \pm 0.015$, Forgetting~$=0.426$) by $+21.3\%$ AA and
$3.3\times$ less forgetting.

The core insight is that quantum expressivity alone (Quantum Naive,
Quantum EWC) does not confer an advantage---but when combined with
Fisher parameter protection and experience replay, the compact quantum
parameter space and Hilbert-space geometry amplify the effectiveness of both
mechanisms, producing a synergistic anti-forgetting system.

Future work will extend QTCL to real quantum hardware, larger benchmarks,
and systematic ablation across circuit depth, qubit count, and rehearsal ratio.
