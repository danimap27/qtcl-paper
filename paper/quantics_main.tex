% ─── Abstract ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
Catastrophic forgetting prevents quantum machine learning models from retaining
previously learned tasks when trained sequentially.
This paper presents Quantum Transfer Continual Learning (QTCL), a hybrid
quantum-classical framework combining a trainable encoder, Variational Quantum
Circuits with task-shared and task-specific parameter banks, Fisher
Information-based Elastic Weight Consolidation computed via adjoint
differentiation, and an experience replay buffer.
On Split-MNIST (5 sequential binary tasks, 3~seeds), QTCL achieves average
accuracy $0.844 \pm 0.012$ and forgetting $0.130 \pm 0.023$, a $+20.8$\,pp
gain and $3.3\times$ forgetting reduction over the best classical baseline.
A noise robustness analysis under the IBM\,Brisbane calibration noise model
shows only a 2.6\,\% accuracy drop, confirming compatibility with near-term
quantum hardware.
\keywords{Quantum Machine Learning \and Continual Learning \and
Variational Quantum Circuits \and Elastic Weight Consolidation \and
Barren Plateaus \and NISQ}
\end{abstract}

% ─── 1. Introduction ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

In many practical settings, a machine learning model must learn a sequence of
tasks over time without access to prior data.
This scenario, known as \emph{continual learning}, is hindered by
\emph{catastrophic forgetting}~\cite{mccloskey1989,goodfellow2013empirical}:
gradient updates for new tasks overwrite parameters encoding past knowledge.
Classical anti-forgetting strategies fall into three families:
regularization~\cite{kirkpatrick2017overcoming,zenke2017continual},
replay~\cite{Rebuffi2017}, and architectural methods.
In practice, combining regularization and replay yields the best
trade-off~\cite{Rebuffi2017}.

Variational Quantum Circuits
(VQCs)~\cite{cerezo2021variational,jerbi2023beyond} are parameterized
circuits suitable for machine learning in the Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum
(NISQ) regime.
Their parameter space is endowed with the \emph{Quantum Fisher Information
Matrix} (QFIM)~\cite{haug2021}, a Riemannian metric capturing the curvature
of the circuit output with respect to parameters.
Applying Fisher-based Elastic Weight Consolidation (EWC) to VQC parameters is
therefore geometrically motivated: it protects the directions of highest
curvature in quantum parameter space.
However, two problems arise.
First, the \emph{barren plateau} phenomenon~\cite{mcclean2018barren,larocca2024review}
causes gradients to vanish exponentially with circuit depth, making recovery
from parameter drift during sequential training hard.
Second, highly expressive ansätze are more prone to barren
plateaus~\cite{holmes2022connecting}, limiting practical circuit depth.

This paper proposes QTCL, which addresses these problems by combining:
(i) QFIM-based EWC to protect quantum parameters geometrically, and
(ii) experience replay to provide gradient anchors preventing parameter drift
into barren plateau regions.
The contributions are as follows.

\noindent\textit{QTCL framework.}
An end-to-end hybrid model with MLP encoder, VQC with task-shared and
task-specific parameter banks, QFIM-based EWC, and experience replay,
evaluated with statistical rigour (3~seeds, 95\,\%~CI).

\noindent\textit{Noise robustness.}
QTCL sustains only a 2.6\,\% accuracy drop under the IBM\,Brisbane
calibration depolarizing noise model, validating near-term hardware compatibility.

\noindent\textit{Ablation study.}
Systematic analysis of EWC strength $\lambda$ and rehearsal ratio $\rho$,
connecting results to barren plateau theory.

% ─── 2. Related Work ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\noindent\textit{Continual learning.}
Kirkpatrick et al.~\cite{kirkpatrick2017overcoming} introduced EWC, penalizing
parameter changes weighted by the diagonal Fisher Information Matrix (FIM).
Zenke et al.~\cite{zenke2017continual} proposed Synaptic Intelligence,
accumulating parameter importance online.
Rebuffi et al.~\cite{Rebuffi2017} introduced rehearsal-based iCaRL.
Parisi et al.~\cite{parisi2019continual} survey the field comprehensively.

\noindent\textit{Variational quantum circuits.}
Cerezo et al.~\cite{cerezo2021variational} review variational quantum algorithms.
The QFIM gives a natural Riemannian metric on quantum circuit parameter
space~\cite{haug2021}.
Holmes et al.~\cite{holmes2022connecting} connect ansatz expressibility to
gradient magnitudes.
McClean et al.~\cite{mcclean2018barren} characterize the barren plateau problem;
Larocca et al.~\cite{larocca2024review} review mitigation strategies.
Wang et al.~\cite{wang2021noise} show that hardware noise can induce barren
plateaus independently of circuit depth.

\noindent\textit{Quantum continual learning.}
Chen~\cite{chen2022quantumcl} studied catastrophic forgetting in VQC classifiers
and proposed quantum-adapted regularization.
QTCL differs in three ways: it computes the FIM exactly via adjoint
differentiation rather than approximating it, combines regularization with
experience replay, and uses a hybrid encoder decoupling classical
pre-processing from the quantum circuit.

% ─── 3. Methodology ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Architecture}
\label{subsec:arch}

A two-layer MLP maps raw 784-dim MNIST inputs to a bounded latent vector:
\[
  \mathbf{z} = \tanh\!\bigl(W_2\,\mathrm{LN}(\mathrm{ReLU}(W_1\mathbf{x}))\bigr)
  \in [-1,+1]^{n_q},
\]
where Layer Normalization (LN) prevents encoder drift across tasks.
The latent vector is encoded via $R_Y(\pi z_i)$ rotations and processed by:
\begin{equation}
  U_\mathrm{ans}(\boldsymbol\theta_s, \boldsymbol\theta_\mathrm{task}^{(t)}) =
  U_\mathrm{SEL}(\boldsymbol\theta_\mathrm{task}^{(t)})\,
  U_\mathrm{SEL}(\boldsymbol\theta_s),
  \label{eq:ansatz}
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol\theta_s$ are \emph{shared} across all tasks,
$\boldsymbol\theta_\mathrm{task}^{(t)}$ are \emph{specific} to task $t$, and
$U_\mathrm{SEL}$ denotes \texttt{StronglyEntanglingLayers} (parameterized
$SU(2)$ rotations with all-to-all entanglement).
Pauli-$Z$ expectations $\langle Z_i\rangle\in[-1,+1]^{n_q}$ feed a linear
classifier.
Default: $n_q=4$, $L_s=2$ shared layers, $L_t=1$ task layer (36 quantum
parameters total).

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\columnwidth]{figures/architecture.pdf}
  \caption{QTCL architecture. The MLP encoder maps 784-dim MNIST inputs to
           a $n_q$-dim bounded latent vector, which is angle-encoded into the
           VQC. The circuit applies a shared layer $U_\mathrm{SEL}(\boldsymbol\theta_s)$
           followed by a task-specific layer $U_\mathrm{SEL}(\boldsymbol\theta_\mathrm{task}^{(t)})$.

\end{figure}

\subsection{Fisher EWC for Hybrid Models}
\label{subsec:ewc}

The QTCL training loss for task $T$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_\mathrm{QTCL} = \mathcal{L}_\mathrm{CE} +
  \frac{\lambda}{2}\sum_{t<T}\sum_i
  \hat{F}_i^{(t)}\!\left(\theta_i - \theta_i^{(t)*}\right)^{\!2},
  \label{eq:ewc}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{CE}$ is cross-entropy, $\hat{F}_i^{(t)}$ is the
estimated diagonal FIM for parameter $i$ after task $t$, and
$\theta_i^{(t)*}$ is the optimal value of parameter $i$ after task $t$.
The diagonal FIM is computed via per-sample backward passes:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{F}_i^{(t)} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^{N}
  \!\left(\frac{\partial\log p(y_n|\mathbf{x}_n,\boldsymbol\theta)}
               {\partial\theta_i}\right)^{\!2},
  \label{eq:fim}
\end{equation}
where gradients through the VQC use adjoint differentiation.
For quantum parameters, the diagonal of $\hat{F}_i$ coincides with the
diagonal of the QFIM under the cross-entropy loss when the model is
well-calibrated~\cite{cerezo2021variational}, providing a geometric
justification: the penalty protects the directions of highest curvature in
quantum Hilbert space.
Classical (encoder) and quantum (VQC) parameters are treated uniformly with
separate strengths $\lambda_C=500$ and $\lambda_Q=200$ to account for their
different curvature scales ($p_Q=36$ vs.\ $p_C\approx25{,}000$ parameters).

\subsection{Training Algorithm}
\label{subsec:alg}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:qtcl} describes the full QTCL procedure.
After each task, the diagonal FIM is computed on a calibration subset,
the parameters are saved as the EWC reference point, and a reservoir
sample~\cite{vitter1985random} of past data is added to the replay buffer
$\mathcal{B}$, which is mixed with new-task data in all subsequent steps.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{QTCL: Fisher EWC + Experience Replay}
\label{alg:qtcl}
\small
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Tasks $\{\mathcal{T}_i\}_{i=1}^T$; $\lambda_Q,\lambda_C$; ratio $\rho$; epochs $E$
\State Init encoder $\phi$, shared $\boldsymbol\theta_s$,
       task params $\boldsymbol\theta_\mathrm{task}^{(t)}$, classifier $W$;
       $\mathcal{B}\!\leftarrow\!\emptyset$; $\hat{\mathbf{F}}\!\leftarrow\!\emptyset$
\For{$t = 1$ to $T$}
  \State Train on $\mathcal{D}_t\cup\mathcal{B}$ with $\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{CE}+\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{EWC}$ for $E$ epochs
  \State Compute $\hat{\mathbf{F}}^{(t)}$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:fim}); save $\boldsymbol\theta^{*(t)}$
  \State $\mathcal{B}\leftarrow\mathcal{B}\cup\mathrm{Reservoir}(\mathcal{D}_t,\lfloor\rho|\mathcal{D}_t|\rfloor)$
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Noise Simulation}
\label{subsec:noise_method}

To assess noise robustness, a depolarizing noise model is constructed from the
calibration data of the IBM\,Brisbane 127-qubit processor, extracted via the
\texttt{FakeBrisbane} backend of \texttt{qiskit-ibm-runtime}.
Single-qubit gates (SX) use per-qubit error rates
$p_\mathrm{1q} \in \{2.43, 2.43, 3.35, 3.43\}\times10^{-4}$
(mean $\bar{p}_\mathrm{1q}=2.91\times10^{-4}$); two-qubit gates (CX) use
$p_\mathrm{2q}=10^{-2}$.
Readout errors are not included.
The model is applied in PennyLane by switching from \texttt{lightning.qubit}
(statevector) to \texttt{default.mixed} (density matrix), which applies after
each gate the depolarizing channel:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{E}_p(\rho) =
  (1-p)\,\rho + \frac{p}{3}\!\sum_{k=1}^{3} \sigma_k\,\rho\,\sigma_k,
  \label{eq:depol}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_k\in\{X,Y,Z\}$ are the Pauli operators and $p\in\{p_\mathrm{1q},
p_\mathrm{2q}\}$ is the gate-specific error rate.
The QTCL model and training loop remain unchanged; only the backend is replaced.

% ─── 4. Results ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Results}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Setup}
\label{subsec:setup}

\noindent\textit{Benchmark.}
Split-MNIST~\cite{lecun1998,zenke2017continual} partitions MNIST into 5
sequential binary tasks: $\mathcal{T}_k$: $(2k{-}2)$ vs $(2k{-}1)$, $k=1\ldots5$.
Each task uses 150 training and 80 test images (balanced).

\noindent\textit{Metrics.}
Let $a_{i,j}$ be test accuracy on task $j$ after training through task $i$.
Average Accuracy (AA), Forgetting (F), Backward Transfer (BWT), and Forward
Transfer (FWT) are defined as:
\begin{align}
  \mathrm{AA}  &= \tfrac{1}{T}\textstyle\sum_j a_{T,j}, \quad
  \mathrm{F}   = \tfrac{1}{T-1}\textstyle\sum_{j<T}(\max_l a_{l,j}-a_{T,j}),
  \label{eq:aa_f}\\
  \mathrm{BWT} &= \tfrac{1}{T-1}\textstyle\sum_{j<T}(a_{T,j}-a_{j,j}), \quad
  \mathrm{FWT} = \tfrac{1}{T-1}\textstyle\sum_{j>1}(a_{j-1,j}-0.5),
  \label{eq:bwt_fwt}
\end{align}
where $0.5$ is the random baseline for binary classification.

\noindent\textit{Methods.}
Five methods are compared:
\textit{Classical Naive} (MLP, no protection);
\textit{Classical EWC} (MLP + EWC, $\lambda_C=500$);
\textit{Quantum Naive} (QTCL architecture, no protection);
\textit{Quantum EWC} (QTCL + EWC, $\lambda_Q=200$, no rehearsal);
\textit{QTCL} (full system, $\rho=0.25$).
All share the same encoder, Adam ($\eta=0.002$), batch size~16, and 25 epochs
per task.

\noindent\textit{Infrastructure.}
All experiments ran on PennyLane \texttt{lightning.qubit} (adjoint
differentiation) on the Hercules HPC cluster of Universidad Pablo de Olavide
(SLURM, 24 cores, 48\,GB RAM).
Results are means $\pm$ std over 3 seeds.

Three patterns stand out from Table~\ref{tab:main}.

\noindent\textit{Rehearsal is the critical component.}
Quantum EWC alone (AA~$=0.617$) gives no significant improvement over Classical
EWC (AA~$=0.636$).
Adding rehearsal yields a $+0.227$ AA jump, the largest single-component gain.
This supports the hypothesis that replay provides the gradient signals needed
to keep parameters out of barren plateau regions~\cite{mcclean2018barren}.

\noindent\textit{Forgetting reduction.}
QTCL reduces forgetting from 0.426 to 0.130, a $3.3\times$ reduction.
Figure~\ref{fig:accmatrix} shows the $5\times5$ accuracy matrix $a[i,j]$:
QTCL maintains near-peak off-diagonal entries throughout sequential training,
whereas Classical EWC shows rapid off-diagonal decay after each task transition.

           through task $i$) for Classical EWC (top) and QTCL (bottom).

\noindent\textit{Forward transfer trade-off.}
While all baselines achieve positive FWT (0.022--0.044), QTCL shows slightly
negative FWT ($-0.006 \pm 0.063$).
The EWC and replay constraints stabilize shared parameters at the cost of
reduced plasticity for zero-shot transfer to unseen tasks.
This trade-off is beneficial overall: backward protection ($3.3\times$ forgetting
reduction) outweighs the marginal loss of forward generalization.

Figure~\ref{fig:ablation} shows the ablation results.
EWC strength $\lambda_Q$ peaks in $[200, 500]$ (AA: 0.853 and 0.869,
indistinguishable over 2 seeds) and degrades sharply for $\lambda_Q\geq1000$,
when over-constrained parameters prevent effective new-task learning.
This optimal range is lower than the classical optimum ($\sim500$),
reflecting the high regularization-per-parameter ratio in the compact VQC space.
Rehearsal ratio $\rho$ shows monotonic improvement up to a broad plateau near
$\rho=0.25$--$0.40$; setting $\rho=0$ reduces to Quantum EWC (AA~$=0.744$,
consistent with the two-seed ablation setting).

QTCL achieves AA $= 0.840\pm0.072$ under the Brisbane noise model, compared

The dominant noise source is the two-qubit gate error ($p_\mathrm{2q}=10^{-2}$);
at the circuit depth used ($L_s=2$, $L_t=1$, 4 qubits), the accumulated error
remains below the threshold for significant accuracy degradation.

% ─── 5. Conclusions ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented QTCL, a hybrid continual learning framework combining
QFIM-based EWC (via adjoint differentiation) with experience replay in a VQC
with task-shared and task-specific parameter banks.
On Split-MNIST (3~seeds, Hercules HPC), QTCL achieves AA~$=0.844\pm0.012$
and F~$=0.130\pm0.023$, a $+20.8$\,pp accuracy gain and $3.3\times$ forgetting
reduction over the best classical baseline.
Noise analysis under IBM\,Brisbane calibration data shows only a 2.6\,\% drop,
confirming NISQ compatibility.

Three findings follow.
Rehearsal is the critical component: QFIM-based EWC alone gives no benefit
over Classical EWC, but the combination yields a $+0.227$ AA gain.
The optimal EWC range $\lambda_Q\in[200,500]$ is lower than for classical
models, reflecting the compact VQC parameter space.
The anti-forgetting constraints trade a small loss of forward transfer for
$3.3\times$ better backward protection.

Limitations include the small dataset scale (150 samples per task), the absence
of readout errors in the noise model, and the use of 2 seeds for noise experiments.
Future work will extend to Split-CIFAR-10 with barren-plateau
mitigation~\cite{larocca2024review}, validate on real IBM Quantum hardware
with Zero-Noise Extrapolation~\cite{wang2021noise}, and study quantum generative
rehearsal as a privacy-preserving alternative to data replay.
